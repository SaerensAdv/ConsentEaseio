4. Major gaps and opportunities

The feature summary listed some known limitations. The most strategically important ones are:
A. No A/B testing

This is a huge opportunity.

If ConsentEase already tracks consent rates, banner shown events, accept/reject/preferences actions, device, country, browser, and Web Vitals, then A/B testing is a natural next step.

A/B testing would let ConsentEase become a consent optimization platform, not just a consent management platform.

Feature idea:
Consent Banner Experiments

Allow users to test:
• Banner position
• Copy
• Button labels
• Button order
• Color contrast
• Accept/reject prominence
• Layout
• Display delay
• Mobile vs desktop layouts
• Language-specific copy

Then show:
• Accept rate
• Reject rate
• Preference-save rate
• Bounce impact
• Web Vitals impact
• Estimated data recovery
• Winning variant

This is one of the clearest ways to stand out.

Positioning:

> Optimize consent rates without dark patterns.

This is important because many CMPs try to increase opt-ins through manipulative design. ConsentEase could instead position itself as ethical optimization:

> “Improve consent rates through clarity, speed, and UX — not tricks.”
---
B. No consent rate benchmarking

This is also a major differentiator.

You already collect events. Over time, ConsentEase could build anonymized benchmarks by:
• Industry
• Country
• Device
• Banner layout
• Website type
• Traffic range
• Language
• Platform: Shopify, WordPress, Wix, custom
• Category: ecommerce, SaaS, local business, agency client, publisher

Feature idea:
Consent Benchmarking

Example dashboard insight:

> “Your analytics consent rate is 48%. Similar ecommerce sites in Belgium average 61%. Your mobile opt-in rate is 19% below benchmark.”

Or:

> “Bottom-right banners on desktop currently perform 12% better than centered modals for similar websites.”

This is much more valuable than raw analytics.

Raw analytics says:

> “Your accept rate is 47%.”

Benchmarking says:

> “Your accept rate is 47%, but it should probably be closer to 58%, and here are the changes most likely to improve it.”

That becomes product intelligence.
---
C. No dashboard AI assistant

Iris is currently public-facing only.

That is useful for support and conversion, but the bigger opportunity is inside the dashboard.

Feature idea:
Iris Compliance Copilot

Inside the authenticated dashboard, Iris could help users understand and fix their setup.

Examples:

> “Why is my Google Consent Mode not working?”

> “Which cookies are uncategorized?”

> “Generate better banner copy for my Belgian ecommerce site.”

> “Explain why my marketing consent rate dropped last week.”

> “What should I do before going live?”

> “Create a client-friendly compliance summary for this website.”

For agencies, this becomes especially useful.

Feature idea:
Client Report Generator

The agency clicks one button and Iris generates:
• What was scanned
• Which cookies were found
• Which categories were configured
• Whether Consent Mode is working
• What risks remain
• What changes were made
• Client-friendly explanation

This could be a very strong agency feature.
---
D. No public REST API

This matters more for agencies, SaaS platforms, and larger customers.

A REST API could enable:
• Creating client sites programmatically
• Fetching consent logs
• Updating banner settings
• Pulling analytics
• Running scans
• Integrating with agency dashboards
• Integrating with website builders
• Custom reporting

But I would not make this the immediate flagship differentiator unless you want to move upmarket.

For SMBs, APIs are not the hook.

For agencies and platform partners, APIs are valuable.

E. No TCF 2.2 support

This is important depending on your target segment.

If you want to serve:
• Publishers
• Media websites
• Ad-heavy websites

Then TCF 2.2 becomes important.

But if your target is:
• SMBs
• Local businesses

Then TCF 2.2 is not necessarily urgent.

I would not make TCF 2.2 the next big differentiator unless you want to enter the publisher/adtech CMP market.

That market is more complex and more competitive.

F. No consent preference center page

Currently preferences are managed through the banner modal only.

A dedicated preference center could improve trust and UX.

Feature idea:

Each site gets a hosted URL like:
