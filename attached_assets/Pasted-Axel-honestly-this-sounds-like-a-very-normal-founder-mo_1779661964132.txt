Axel, honestly: this sounds like a very normal founder moment.

You have something that works, you’ve had conversations, people show interest, agencies see enough value to keep talking, but you don’t yet fully trust the platform/positioning enough to push it hard. That “something feels off” feeling is often not because the product is bad — it’s usually because the commercial story, pricing, audience, or delivery model is not fully locked in yet.

And about the COP idea: I still like it, but I would not jump into building an expensive agent-heavy “Consent Operations Platform” right now. Not yet.

The smarter move is probably:

> Use COP as positioning and product direction first — not as a fully built product immediately.

Meaning: you don’t need to build the full agent-powered operations layer tomorrow. You can start by packaging what you already have differently, then manually/semiautomatically deliver some of the “operations” value for a few agencies or clients. If it sells repeatedly, then you automate.

The “something feels off” may be positioning, not product

A lot of CMPs look similar from the outside.

If ConsentEase says:

> “Cookie banner, consent logging, cookie scanner, Google Consent Mode v2, multilingual, policies…”

Then agencies might think:

> “Okay, nice, but Cookiebot, CookieYes, Complianz, iubenda, Usercentrics, etc. already exist.”

Even if your product is good, the mental category is crowded.

So the issue might not be: “ConsentEase doesn’t work.”

It might be:

> “ConsentEase does not yet have a strong enough reason why an agency should choose it, resell it, and standardize on it.”

That’s different.

For agencies, the strongest reasons are usually not “best compliance platform.”

They care about:
• Easy deployment
• Good pricing/margins
• White-label or agency branding
• Client management
• Simple billing
• Recurring revenue
• Fewer support headaches
• Done-for-you setup options
• Google Consent Mode support
• A reason to contact all existing clients
• A reason to upsell maintenance packages

So ConsentEase may need to become less:

> “A CMP for websites”

And more:

> “An agency consent revenue system.”

That’s likely more commercially powerful for your current path.

The agency cooperation angle could be your wedge

You said something important:

> “Often it led to selling them in my place. I want to make the idea and let people do the work.”

That means your natural growth motion may be channel/partner-led, not direct SaaS self-serve.

That is fine. Actually, for this kind of product, agencies can be a very good channel.

But then the product and offer need to be built for agencies first.

Not necessarily technically, but commercially.
Agencies need this to be painfully clear:
1. What do they sell?

Example:

> “GDPR Cookie Banner + Google Consent Mode v2 Setup”

or:

> “Consent Compliance Package for your website clients”

or:

> “Privacy & Consent Maintenance Plan”
2. How much do they charge?

You can give them suggested pricing.

Example:
• One-time setup: €250–€750
• Monthly maintenance: €15–€75/client
• Premium consent/analytics health monitoring: €99+/month
3. How much do they pay you?

Example:
• Agency pays ConsentEase €X/month per domain
• Agency charges client €Y/month
• Agency keeps margin
4. Who does the work?

Options:
• Agency does all setup
• You do first setup, agency manages client
• You offer done-for-you onboarding as add-on
• Hybrid model
5. What happens if support is needed?

Agencies will worry about this. They don’t want to sell something and then get stuck debugging consent mode, GTM, cookies, and angry clients.

So your partner proposal should clearly define support boundaries.

Possible cooperation models

Here are the main models you could consider.
---
Model 1: Referral partner

They refer clients to ConsentEase. You sell and invoice.

They get commission.
Pros
• Simple
• You control customer relationship
• Less support confusion
Cons
• Agencies may be less motivated
• They may not want you owning the client relationship

Best if they don’t want to do implementation.
---
Model 2: Reseller/agency plan

They buy domains/seats from you at agency pricing and resell to clients.
Pros
• They can package it as their service
• You get partner distribution
• Recurring revenue
Cons
• You may lose direct client relationship
• Need clear support model
• Pricing/margins need to work

Best if they are serious about selling.
---
Model 3: White-label

They sell it under their own brand.
Pros
• Very attractive to agencies
• Could scale through partners
Cons
• More product complexity
• More support complexity
• Your brand disappears
• Can create custom demands

Best only if they bring volume or pay well.
---
Model 4: Done-for-you implementation partner

They sell the package; you handle implementation.
Pros
• Easier for them to sell
• Quality stays high
• You learn client needs
Cons
• Service-heavy
• Harder to scale
• Can drain time

Best in early validation, but price accordingly.
---
Model 5: Hybrid partner package

This may be the best for you right now.

Example:

> Agency sells ConsentEase to their clients. Agency invoices the client. ConsentEase provides platform access, onboarding, training, and optional implementation support. Agency receives partner pricing based on number of domains.

This gives flexibility without overcommitting.
---
What I would avoid agreeing to too quickly

Be cautious with:
1. Exclusivity

If they ask for exclusivity in a region/sector, avoid it unless they commit to serious minimum volume.

Example:

> “We can discuss exclusivity if there is a minimum commitment of X active domains within Y months.”
2. Heavy custom features

If they ask for custom stuff, ask:

> “Is this required for your first 5 clients, or is it nice-to-have?”

Do not build for hypothetical agency needs.
3. Very low pricing

If they want very low prices but no volume guarantee, that’s risky.
4. Unlimited support

Agencies may accidentally make you their unpaid privacy/tracking department.

Set support boundaries.
5. Legal responsibility

Be careful not to position ConsentEase as replacing legal advice.

Use language like:

> “ConsentEase provides tools to help manage consent and compliance workflows, but does not provide legal advice.”
